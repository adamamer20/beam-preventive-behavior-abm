Designing effective epidemic policy is fundamentally a counterfactual problem: it requires anticipating how preventive behaviour would change under alternative interventions. Agent-based models (ABMs) offer a natural framework for this task, but their usefulness depends critically on how individual behavioural rules are specified and validated. This thesis develops a survey-grounded pipeline for building and evaluating a behavioural epidemic simulator, with a focus on preventive behaviour as a structured decision architecture.

The empirical basis is a 2024 multi-country survey of approximately 22,246 respondents from Italy, Spain, France, Germany, Hungary, and the United Kingdom. I first reduce a high-dimensional questionnaire to a compact set of theory-guided behavioural engines, including perceived disease stakes, vaccine-related disposition and risk perceptions, institutional trust, legitimacy, norms, constraints, moral orientation, and habit. Using these blocks, I estimate reference equations for multiple preventive outcomes and derive perturbation-based empirical benchmarks that summarise how behaviour is expected to respond when specific levers shift.

I then evaluate large language models (LLMs) as candidate behavioural components in two tasks: mapping fixed survey-grounded profiles into preventive actions, and updating psychological profile variables under controlled perturbations. The results show a clear asymmetry. In baseline tasks, LLMs often recover broad ranking structure, but their predictions are compressed toward the mean. Under perturbation, their responses are frequently mis-scaled, overly diffuse, and insufficiently contained to the intended behavioural dimensions. These properties make current off-the-shelf LLMs unreliable as core update mechanisms for disciplined counterfactual simulation.

Guided by these findings, the final contribution is an ABM that keeps behaviour anchored to the survey-grounded empirical relationships while adding a transparent dynamic layer based on persistence, social influence, and epidemic feedback. The simulations show that intervention effects differ not only in immediate magnitude but in dynamic signature: norm-based interventions can accumulate and persist through reinforcement, credibility-repair interventions produce broader but gradual shifts, and salience shocks are sharper yet less durable. The thesis therefore argues that credible behavioural policy simulation requires micro-level empirical anchoring, controlled perturbation validation, and explicit separation between representational flexibility and behaviourally disciplined updating.
