3  Methodology

This chapter documents the survey data, preprocessing, and modelling inputs used in later chapters; Figure fig-thesis-pipeline provides a visual map.

3.1 Index construction and measurement quality

Where possible, conceptually related items were aggregated by taking the mean across non-missing responses. Formally, for respondent \(i\) and an index built from items \(j \in J_i\) with observed (non-missing) responses \(x_{ij}\), the index is

\[ \text{Index}_i = \frac{1}{\lvert J_i \rvert} \sum_{j \in J_i} x_{ij}, \]

where \(J_i\) is the set of items with non-missing responses for that respondent. In practice, no additional minimum-item requirement is imposed beyond this available-item rule, so indices may be based on as few as one observed item; I therefore report the percentage coverage for each index and treat low coverage as a signal that missingness reduces the effective sample size for that composite.

For multi-item composites intended to summarise a common latent construct, internal consistency was assessed using Cronbach’s alpha (\(\alpha\)), which captures the average covariance among items relative to the variance of their composite score (Cronbach 1951; Cortina 1993). It is reported as a descriptive reliability diagnostic rather than as evidence of unidimensionality or construct validity.

For composites that are formative by design, such as the 5C pro-vaccine index, \(\alpha\) is reported for transparency but is not used as a criterion for scale adequacy. No internal-consistency statistic is computed for aggregates that pool attributes of heterogeneous entities (such as household and social-contact summaries (cohabitants and non-cohabitants) or media-use duration baskets) since these variables are deterministic summaries rather than psychometric scales.

3.1.1 Derived household and social-contact variables

The survey contains detailed information on up to ten cohabitants and up to ten non-cohabiting close contacts, including their age, COVID-19 vaccination status, and history of COVID-19 infection. To avoid an explosion of highly sparse columns, I summarise this information into a small set of continuous variables:

  • Average age of cohabitants/non-cohabitants
  • Average number of COVID vaccine doses among cohabitants/non-cohabitants
  • Average number of COVID infections among cohabitants/non-cohabitants

3.1.2 Media use and information diet

Time spent with different online and social media is summarised into two duration indices:

  • Duration of Informational Media averages reported time on informational platforms such as Wikipedia, YouTube and Reddit.
  • Duration of Social Media averages time on social platforms (Instagram, Facebook, Telegram, Twitter/X, LinkedIn, TikTok).

3.1.3 Composite psychological indices

5C pro-vaccine index

To obtain a summary measure of pro-vaccine psychological orientation, I construct the 5C pro-vaccine index (\(\alpha \approx 0.72\)) as the mean of four recoded 5C components:

  • 5C Confidence
  • 5C Complacency (reverse-coded)
  • 5C Constraints (reverse-coded)
  • 5C Collective Responsibility

Risk, safety, fear and comfort

Perceptions of infection risk and vaccine risk are captured using multi-item averages:

  • Average perceived infection likelihood across COVID-19, seasonal flu, measles and meningitis (\(\alpha \approx 0.74\)).
  • Average perceived severity if infected across COVID-19 and flu (\(\alpha \approx 0.76;\ \text{coverage}\ 92.8\%\)).
  • Average disease fear across COVID-19, seasonal flu, measles and meningitis (\(\alpha \approx 0.80\)).

Although baseline prevalence differs sharply across pathogens, the perceived infection likelihood composite is interpreted as a dispositional tendency to perceive infectious diseases as likely in general (rather than as a calibrated estimate of objective infection probabilities).

The perceived severity index is likewise treated as a general severity sensitivity: how strongly respondents expect serious consequences conditional on infection, rather than a disease-specific clinical assessment.

For vaccines:

  • Average perceived vaccine risk across COVID-19, flu, measles and meningitis vaccines (\(\alpha \approx 0.84\)).
  • Average vaccine fear captures reported fear of the same four vaccines (\(\alpha \approx 0.87\)).

COVID-19 vaccine rollout scepticism

To capture scepticism about the safety, motives, and mandate-related aspects of the COVID-19 vaccination campaign, I construct a COVID-19 vaccine rollout scepticism composite (\(\alpha \approx 0.77\)) by averaging agreement with six statements covering safety concerns, perceived insufficient testing, side-effect worries, opposition to mandates, distrust in pharmaceutical motives, and preference for natural immunity.

Progressive attitudes and social norms

I construct a Progressive attitudes index (\(\alpha \approx 0.52\)) which averages agreement with:

  • concern about climate harm,
  • support for LGBT adoption rights,
  • tolerance towards immigration.

Perceived vaccine-importance norms are measured separately as single items capturing how important respondents believe vaccination is to their family, friends, and colleagues.

Three analogous progressive norms bundles capture perceived social climate among friends (\(\alpha \approx 0.32;\ \text{coverage}\ 87.2\%\)), family (\(\alpha \approx 0.28;\ \text{coverage}\ 87.4\%\)) and colleagues (\(\alpha \approx 0.26;\ \text{coverage}\ 56.8\%\)) by averaging the same items within each reference group.

The progressive attitudes composite has a modest internal consistency, as expected given its deliberate coverage of several policy domains. It should be interpreted as a broad value gradient rather than a narrow latent trait. Likewise, the progressive norms bundles are treated as descriptive composites (heterogeneous by design), and relatively low Cronbach’s alpha values for these bundles are not taken as evidence of measurement failure but as a reflection of their intentionally broad content.

For vaccination specifically, I also combine the three vaccine-importance norm items into a single Perceived vaccine norms index, defined as their mean ( \(\alpha \approx 0.91;\ \text{coverage}\ 95.1\%\) ). I do so because these three items display very high internal consistency, indicating that perceived vaccine importance is largely shared across respondents’ close reference groups rather than being sharply group-specific.

Finally, to summarise a broader moral–progressive orientation that links progressive policy attitudes to individualising moral concerns (care and fairness), I construct a higher-order composite by averaging the Progressive attitudes index with the two individualising morality constructs (moral relevance and moral agreement). The resulting composite is reported with its internal-consistency diagnostic ( \(\alpha \approx 0.53\) ).

Protective behaviour bundle

Protective behaviour index (\(\alpha \approx 0.78\)) summarises three non-pharmaceutical behavioural items:

  • wearing a mask in crowded places when symptomatic,
  • staying at home when symptomatic,
  • wearing a mask when “pressure” (infection risk) is high.

3.1.4 Trust indices

Trust in information sources is summarised at three levels:

  • Trust in traditional media (\(\alpha \approx 0.86;\ \text{coverage}\ 73.8\%\)) Mean of trust in printed press, television, and radio as sources of vaccine information.

  • Trust in online information (\(\alpha \approx 0.73;\ \text{coverage}\ 72.6\%\)) Average trust in online news websites, Wikipedia, YouTube and podcasts as vaccine information sources.

  • Trust in social media (\(\alpha \approx 0.66;\ \text{coverage}\ 74.1\%\)) Average trust in Facebook, Instagram, Twitter/X, TikTok, Telegram and LinkedIn.

Finally, Institutional trust index (\(\alpha \approx 0.91\)) aggregates trust in:

  • local and national governments,
  • general practitioners,
  • local and national health authorities,
  • the WHO,

3.1.5 Moral foundations indices

Moral values were measured using an adapted version of the Moral Foundations Questionnaire (MFQ), which distinguishes between five moral foundations: care, fairness, loyalty, authority, and purity (Haidt and Joseph 2004; Graham et al. 2011). Following standard practice, I construct separate indices for relevance and agreement items within each foundation, and further aggregate them into higher-order individualizing (care, fairness) and binding (loyalty, authority, purity) dimensions. These higher-order composites are used in the main analyses, while foundation-specific subscales are treated as exploratory.

For each foundation, I construct:

  • Relevance subscales each averaging two items about how morally relevant a given consideration is (e.g., preventing emotional suffering, respecting authority):
    • Care relevance (\(\alpha \approx 0.71\)),
    • Fairness relevance (\(\alpha \approx 0.70\)),
    • Loyalty relevance (\(\alpha \approx 0.44\)),
    • Authority relevance (\(\alpha \approx 0.57\)),
    • Purity relevance (\(\alpha \approx 0.65\))
  • Agreement subscales each averaging two items about agreement with value statements:
    • Care agreement (\(\alpha \approx 0.59\)),
    • Fairness agreement (\(\alpha \approx 0.69\)),
    • Loyalty agreement (\(\alpha \approx 0.48\)),
    • Authority agreement (\(\alpha \approx 0.31\)),
    • Purity agreement (\(\alpha \approx 0.51\))

To align with standard MFQ practice, I then form individualizing and binding higher-order indices: - Relevance - Individualizing index (\(\alpha \approx 0.79\)) averages care and fairness relevance, - Relevance - Binding index (\(\alpha \approx 0.80\)) averages loyalty, authority and purity relevance; - Agreement - Individualizing index (\(\alpha \approx 0.72\)) averages care and fairness agreement, - Agreement - Binding index (\(\alpha \approx 0.70\)) averages loyalty, authority and purity agreement.

As is typical for two-item subscales, some foundation-specific reliability estimates are modest. The main analyses therefore rely primarily on the higher-order individualizing and binding composites, with foundation-level subscales interpreted as exploratory detail rather than as standalone, high-precision latent constructs.

3.2 Statistical modelling methods

sec-03-decision-function moves from screening to multivariate backbone modelling (coordinated GLMs), estimating regression families that compress the gradients in sec-04-statistical-analysis into a small set of portable engines and decision rules for profiling, micro-validation, and agent-based simulation. Predictors are introduced in a conceptual order from upstream context to downstream outcomes: demographic and health background, social embedding, and information environments relate to psychological antecedents (trust, risk perceptions, moral and descriptive norms, and 5C dimensions), which in turn relate to attitudes (e.g., perceived importance of vaccines) and behavioural outcomes (e.g., willingness, uptake, routine flu vaccination, and non-pharmaceutical protective actions). This ordering determines how blocks are grouped and which intermediate constructs are modelled before behaviour.

3.2.1 Model family and outcome types

The outcomes span binary indicators, ordinal Likert-type items, and (where applicable) continuous composite measures. To match these measurement scales, I use generalised linear models (GLMs):

  • Binary outcomes (e.g., ever vs never vaccinated; adoption indicators) are modelled with logistic regression.
  • Ordinal outcomes (Likert-type responses) are modelled with ordinal logistic regression (proportional-odds specification for interpretability).
  • Continuous outcomes / indices are modelled with linear regression when the modelling representation is approximately continuous.

Country fixed effects are included in all pooled models unless otherwise stated to absorb baseline cross-national differences while preserving the within-sample structure of associations between predictors and outcomes.

Before estimation, all continuous and ordinal predictors are standardised using pooled z-scores (mean 0, SD 1) computed on the estimation sample. Binary indicators remain coded as 0/1, and categorical predictors are entered as dummy-coded contrasts with an explicit reference category. Coefficient magnitudes are therefore directly comparable only among the standardised continuous/ordinal predictors; binary and categorical coefficients are interpreted on their own contrasts.

Results are reported on the natural scale implied by each link function (log-odds for logistic and ordinal models; linear coefficients for continuous outcomes), alongside uncertainty summaries. The modelling outputs are then used to (i) motivate a reduced “backbone” set of domains that repeatedly explain variation across prevention outcomes and (ii) define simulation-ready components that can be transported into the clustering and ABM stages.

3.2.2 Block-structured specifications with LOBO/LOBI importance scores

To avoid ad-hoc covariate choices and keep comparisons interpretable, I use a block-structured specification scheme. A block is a pre-defined group of predictors capturing a semantically coherent domain (e.g., demographics and health background, institutional trust, 5C antecedents, risk perceptions, social norms, information exposure, and social contact structure). Blocks are defined once and reused across outcomes, so model variants are obtained by adding or removing entire domains rather than individual variables.

This design improves interpretability, nested specifications correspond to substantive questions (e.g., “what changes once I add trust?”), and enables domain-level importance analysis without relying only on coefficient inspection. For each outcome, block contribution is summarised using two complementary diagnostics, both reported with a change-in-fit measure and a complexity-adjusted criterion. Fit changes are measured as \(\Delta\) pseudo-\(R^2\) for logistic/ordinal models (McFadden’s pseudo-\(R^2\)) and \(\Delta R^2\) for OLS (McFadden 1974) Complexity adjustment is measured with \(\Delta\)BIC (Bayesian information criterion) (Schwarz 1978). All quantities are oriented so that larger positive values indicate greater block contribution.

  • LOBO (Leave-One-Block-Out): starting from the reference model (the full block-saturated specification for the outcome), each block \(b\) is removed in turn. Importance is summarised by the loss in fit \[ \Delta \text{fit}^{\text{LOBO}}_b = \text{fit}_{\text{full}} - \text{fit}_{-b}, \] and by the increase in BIC \[ \Delta \text{BIC}^{\text{LOBO}}_b = \text{BIC}_{-b} - \text{BIC}_{\text{full}}, \] where positive values indicate that removing the block worsens the model.

  • LOBI (Leave-One-Block-In): starting from a minimal baseline (typically country fixed effects and socio-demography), each block \(b\) is added alone to assess its standalone contribution. Gains are summarised by \[ \Delta \text{fit}^{\text{LOBI}}_b = \text{fit}_{+b} - \text{fit}_{\text{base}}, \] and by the BIC improvement \[ \Delta \text{BIC}^{\text{LOBI}}_b = \text{BIC}_{\text{base}} - \text{BIC}_{+b}, \] where positive values indicate that introducing the block improves fit net of complexity.

3.3 LLM behavioural micro-validation

sec-06-llm-micro-validation tests whether large language models reproduce the directional regularities identified in the modelling chapter under controlled, on-manifold counterfactual manipulations. The aim is not to “predict the survey”, but to assess whether an LLM can act as a plausible behavioural engine that preserves the same qualitative gradients estimated in sec-03-decision-function.

Micro-validation targets two ABM components. The choice-policy track treats the LLM as a mapping from respondent state \(z\) to an action or attitude output \(y\) and tests counterfactual fidelity under lever perturbations against the backbone models. The belief-update track treats the LLM as a state-transition kernel \((z,s)\mapsto z'\) in response to informational or social signals and tests whether updates are bounded, directionally plausible, and behaviourally consistent when propagated through the fixed decision rules.

\[ \begin{aligned} \textbf{Choice policy:}\quad & \hat y = \pi(z, x) && \text{(validate via ICE contrasts under lever perturbations in } x\text{)}\\ \textbf{Belief update:}\quad & z' = \mathcal{U}(z, s),\qquad \hat y'=\pi(z', x) && \text{(validate update realism + downstream action consistency)} \end{aligned} \]

3.3.1 Anchor prototypes for on-manifold evaluation and sampling

A recurring problem in downstream validation and simulation is that the behavioural state space \(Z_{\text{core}}\) is high-dimensional and strongly correlated. If we probe an LLM (or initialise agents) using arbitrary or independently perturbed covariates, we quickly create unrealistic combinations. Earlier drafts explored clustering-based summaries, but the resulting partitions were not sufficiently stable to justify a fixed typology (details are relegated to sec-appendix-clustering). Instead, I adopt anchor prototypes (a small set of observed respondent profiles) which serve three operational roles: coverage (tiling the empirical manifold at manageable cost), locality (defining country-stratified neighbourhoods for resampling complete observed profiles), and discipline (using on-manifold reference states for lever perturbations with explicit off-manifold diagnostics).

Let \(z_i\in\mathbb{R}^p\) denote respondent \(i\)’s vector of core behavioural drivers (the same engine space used throughout modelling, micro-validation, and simulation). To remove country-specific baselines while preserving within-country structure, each driver is normalised as \[ z'_{ij} = \frac{z_{ij} - \bar z_{c(i)j}}{s_j}, \]

where \(\bar z_{c(i)j}\) is the mean of driver \(j\) in respondent \(i\)’s country and \(s_j\) is a global scale (the pooled standard deviation computed after within-country demeaning). Drivers with very high missingness are excluded, and remaining missing values are imputed only for anchor construction and distance geometry, using a within-country kNN imputer so that distances are well-defined. Downstream analyses and resampling always use observed respondent profiles (no global imputation). This mirrors the intuition of donor-based (hot-deck / nearest-neighbour) approaches used in survey settings, where plausible values are borrowed from similar respondents (Andridge and Little 2010).

Distances are computed in a PCA-reduced representation of \(z'\) for geometry only: PCA is fit on the imputed \(z'\) matrix, components are retained to reach a variance target (\(0.97\) in the main pipeline), and all interpretation and reporting remain on the original construct scales (Jolliffe 2002).

Anchors are selected as observed medoids in distance space using k-medoids with multiple random starts (Kaufman and Rousseeuw 2005). To avoid anchors that behave as de facto country outliers, selection is regularised with a globality constraint: each anchor must have non-trivial support in at least \(C_{\min}\) countries. Support in country \(c\) is defined as \(\pi_{k\mid c}\ge \pi_{\min}\) (main configuration: \(C_{\min}=3\), \(\pi_{\min}=0.05\)).

Using a preliminary temperature \(\tau_{\text{init}}\), I compute soft memberships as \[ w_{ik} = \frac{\exp\left(-d(\tilde z_i, a_k)/\tau\right)}{\sum_{\ell=1}^K \exp\left(-d(\tilde z_i, a_\ell)/\tau\right)}, \qquad \sum_{k=1}^K w_{ik}=1, \] and define the country prevalence of anchor \(k\) by \[ \pi_{k\mid c} = \frac{1}{n_c}\sum_{i: c(i)=c} w_{ik}. \] Among candidate k-medoids solutions, the final anchor set minimises the average within-cluster distance augmented by the globality penalty, favouring solutions that are both locally tight and cross-nationally supported.

After anchors are selected, memberships are recomputed using a final temperature \(\tau\). In the main configuration, \(\tau\) is calibrated to match a target effective number of anchors per respondent, \[ k_{\text{eff}}(i) = \frac{1}{\sum_{k=1}^K w_{ik}^2}, \] so that typical respondents are represented by a sparse mixture rather than being pushed into near-hard assignments or spread uniformly across all anchors.

In practice, I set the initial kernel temperature \(\tau_{\text{init}}\) to a typical local distance scale, estimated as the median 10-nearest-neighbour distance in PCA geometry space (computed on a random sample of \(n=5000\) respondents). I then tune the final temperature \(\tau\) so that the median effective number of anchors is \(k_{\text{eff}}=2\), yielding a representation where a typical respondent is captured by roughly two anchors.

Anchors then induce country-stratified neighbourhoods. For each anchor \(k\) and country \(c\), let \(\mathcal{N}_{k,c}\) denote the set of nearest respondents within country \(c\) to anchor \(a_k\) in distance space. These neighbourhoods support resampling of complete respondent profiles in \(Z_{\text{core}}\), preserving cross-domain correlations (e.g., trust–risk–norm coupling) by construction. For LLM micro-validation, I assemble a compact evaluation set by sampling a bounded number of respondents from each \(\mathcal{N}_{k,c}\) while enforcing global uniqueness (so the same respondent is not repeatedly reused across anchor–country cells). The result is an evaluation grid that remains diverse across anchors and countries, yet stays computationally tractable.

Perturbed-profile plausibility (off-manifold rate).

Because counterfactual profiles (related to sec-03-llm-micro-validation) edit only a subset of coordinates, they can in principle create implausible joint combinations even when baseline profiles are sampled from the respondent manifold. I therefore flag “off-manifold” perturbations using a nearest-neighbour distance check in the same anchoring feature space (and geometry) used for prototype construction. Within each country \(s\), I estimate a local distance scale by computing each respondent’s distance to its nearest other respondent, and set a plausibility threshold \(t_s\) as the \(p=0.95\) quantile of that distribution. After constructing the low/high perturbed profiles by replacing only the target coordinate (to the within-country \(q_{25}/q_{75}\) on the normalised scale), a perturbed point is flagged as off-manifold if its nearest-neighbour distance to the observed respondent set exceeds \(t_s\). The off-manifold rate is the mean flag rate within each lever-country cell.

3.3.2 Models and prompting strategies

To separate variation due to model capability from variation due to prompt formulation, the evaluation varies both the LLM configuration and the prompt representation while holding the underlying respondent profile state fixed. Each prompting strategy is generated by a deterministic renderer from the same numeric profile state, so differences across strategies reflect the model’s sensitivity to representation rather than prompt-generation noise. Across strategies and models, the target outcome, response scale, perturbation design, and parsing rules are held constant.

  1. Data-only (structured) prompting: the model receives the respondent profile as a structured attribute vector and returns the target attitude or behaviour on the predefined outcome scale.

  2. Narrative profile rendering (third-person, lossy): the same profile is deterministically rendered into a short vignette in the third person (e.g., “This person is…”). The rendering is intentionally compressive to test whether directional regularities persist when the model is not given fine-grained numeric inputs.

  3. Narrative profile rendering (first-person, self-description framing): the same vignette is expressed in the first person (e.g., “You are…”) and framed as a self-description authored by the assistant. This tests whether a self-referential stance yields higher self-consistency and reduces “regression-to-the-mean” tendencies relative to third-person descriptions, while keeping content identical up to deterministic rewriting.

  4. Three-expert decomposition (delta-over-baseline): The model first produces a baseline prediction \(\hat y_0\) from the data-only representation. It is then prompted as three perspectives aligned with the decision architecture; each expert outputs a signed delta \(\delta_k\) (in outcome-scale units) relative to \(\hat y_0\), using only evidence from its channel. The final prediction is obtained deterministically: \[ \hat y = \mathrm{Proj}_{\mathcal{Y}}\left(\hat y_0 + \sum_{k=1}^{3}\delta_k\right), \] where \(\mathrm{Proj}_{\mathcal{Y}}\) deterministically enforces the outcome’s response scale. For the choice task, the perspectives correspond to stakes/exposure, norms–legitimacy, and constraints; for belief updating, they correspond to institutions/safety–legitimacy, social influence, and stakes–constraints.

  5. Paired contrast prompting (low/high and low–mid–high; only for track A): profiles at different perturbation levels are presented jointly and the model returns the corresponding outputs. This yields a lower-variance estimate of directional effects by making the counterfactual contrast explicit in-context.

Apart from the last one, the same prompt families are reused across both tracks, with track-specific output schemas (choice outputs vs construct updates) and track-specific inputs described in sec-03-choice-microvalidation and sec-03-belief-update-microvalidation.

The suite is run across multiple open-weight model configurations to test robustness to scale and deliberation settings. As a separate sensitivity check, I vary sampling temperature on a small subset to assess robustness to response stochasticity. The main workhorse model is openai/gpt-oss-20b with reasoning effort set to medium. To probe sensitivity to additional deliberation at fixed scale, I also evaluate openai/gpt-oss-20b with reasoning effort set to high. To test scaling effects within the same family, I include openai/gpt-oss-120b. Finally, to check generalisation across model families, I include qwen/qwen3-32b.

3.3.3 Shared evaluation machinery

Declared common outcome scale

All predictions are evaluated on a declared, common numeric scale. For continuous GLMs, the reference prediction is the fitted linear predictor. For binary and ordinal GLMs, the reference prediction is the expected score \(E[Y]\) computed from predicted category probabilities; in sensitivity analyses I also report thresholded probabilities \(P(Y\ge \tau)\) for policy-relevant cutoffs. LLM outputs are forced onto the same scale via constrained JSON answers, and a deterministic projection operator \(\mathrm{Proj}_{\mathcal{Y}}(\cdot)\) enforces the outcome’s allowed response range; categorical responses are mapped to identical ordinal codes prior to aggregation.

Calibration layer for LLM outputs

Empirically, LLM predictions preserve rank order more reliably than absolute levels. To avoid conflating these properties, I treat the LLM as a scoring function and add a separate, train-only calibration layer that maps the LLM scale onto the survey scale. Calibration is always fit on the training split and applied out-of-fold via cross-fitting, so every evaluation prediction is calibrated by a model that never saw that row. The calibration map is constrained to be monotone so that it adjusts levels without breaking ordering.

For binary outcomes, I use a Platt-style logit calibration with a country intercept and global slope: \[ \operatorname{logit}(p_{\text{true}})=a_c + b\,\operatorname{logit}(p_{\text{llm}}). \] For ordinal/continuous outcomes, I use a monotone rescaling with a country intercept, fit via isotonic regression: \[ \hat y_{\text{cal}}=f_c\!\left(\hat y_{\text{llm}}\right),\qquad f_c\ \text{monotone}. \]

LLM sampling protocol

Unless otherwise stated, each prompt instance is evaluated using fixed, recommended model-family decoding settings and averaged over \(K=4\) independent generations. For openai/gpt-oss-20b and openai/gpt-oss-120b, I use temperature \(=1.0\) and top-\(p=1.0\) (with no top-\(k\) truncation). For qwen/qwen3-32b, I use temperature \(=0.6\), top-\(p=0.95\), top-\(k=20\) and min-\(p=0\), consistent with the recommended non-degenerate sampling regime for that model family. As a sensitivity check, I run a temperature sweep for gpt-oss-20b over (\({0,\ 0.2,\ 0.4,\ 0.7}\)). Point estimates (baseline levels and perturbation contrasts) are stable across temperatures, while higher temperature primarily increases within-cell dispersion rather than shifting the mean; all main results therefore use the default temperature setting and report uncertainty via bootstrap over generations and reference profiles.

Malformed JSON or refusals are retried up to three times to obtain valid outputs; persistent failures are recorded and reported as a non-compliance rate, \[ r_{\text{noncomp}} = \frac{N_{\text{fail}}}{N_{\text{attempt}}}. \]

Two evaluation regimes (levels vs effects). I distinguish (i) unperturbed score alignment on observed profiles, which checks whether the LLM can represent baseline variation on the declared outcome scale, from (ii) perturbation-effect alignment, which is the ABM-relevant criterion and tests whether the LLM responds appropriately to targeted counterfactual lever shifts holding the rest of the profile fixed.

3.3.4 Choice-policy micro-validation

As a baseline diagnostic, I evaluate LLM outputs on unperturbed (observed) profiles to assess response-scale compliance, calibration quality, and whether the model preserves rank order on the declared outcome scale. These checks are informative but not sufficient for ABM use.

Predictive accuracy on unperturbed profiles is not sufficient because many psychological engines co-vary with socio-demographics, political orientation, and country context. An LLM can therefore match baseline outcome levels by exploiting shortcut cues rather than the intended construct mapping; it can be “right for the wrong reasons”. For instance, if perceived vaccine risk is correlated with willingness through education or country composition in the cross-section, a model may appear accurate on unperturbed profiles while responding weakly (or with the wrong sign) when we intervene ceteris paribus on perceived vaccine risk or institutional trust within the same profile.

Unperturbed mismatches are also weakly diagnostic about why an LLM fails. Deviations from the backbone can arise because relevant determinants are missing or compressed in the prompt representation, because the output scale is mis-calibrated, or because the model applies different behavioural logic even when the required information is present. Conversely, good baseline fit does not imply correct construct-to-action mapping. For ABM purposes, the key property is counterfactual fidelity: outputs should move appropriately when a specific behavioural lever is shifted while holding the rest of the profile fixed.

Unperturbed baseline diagnostics (rank and level)

For each outcome \(Y\), the unperturbed benchmark evaluates predictions on observed profiles \(z\), with the LLM output aggregated as the mean over \(K\) generations under strategy \(s\), written \(\hat y_{\text{llm}}^{(s)}(z)\). I report two generic accuracy summaries that I reuse later for perturbation contrasts:

  • Rank discrimination (Gini / concordance). Let \(C\) denote a tie-aware concordance index computed over pairs \((i,j)\) as the proportion of correctly ordered outcome pairs under the model scores (ties receive \(0.5\) credit): \[ C=\frac{n_{\text{conc}}+0.5\,n_{\text{ties}}}{n_{\text{pairs}}},\qquad G=2C-1. \] Here \(G=1\) indicates perfect ranking and \(G=0\) indicates chance-level ranking.

  • Level accuracy (MAE skill). Let \(\mathrm{MAE}(f)\) be the mean absolute error of model \(f\) on the declared outcome scale, and let \(f_0\) be a simple baseline predictor (e.g., an unconditional mean on the appropriate scale). I report \[ \mathrm{skill}=1-\frac{\mathrm{MAE}(f)}{\mathrm{MAE}(f_0)}, \] so \(\mathrm{skill}=1\) is perfect, \(\mathrm{skill}=0\) matches the baseline, and negative values are worse than the baseline.

Perturbation-based validation (primary): ICE contrasts under matched shocks

I treat perturbation-based validation as primary. For each outcome \(Y\) and validation target \(X\), I test whether an LLM preserves the direction and relative strength of the behavioural gradients estimated by the fitted statistical models by applying controlled counterfactual perturbations.

For a fixed profile \(z\) and model \(f\), the individual conditional expectation (ICE) curve is \[ \mathrm{ICE}_f(x;z)=f(x,z). \] Perturbation effects are discrete ICE contrasts computed on a two- or three-point grid (Goldstein et al. 2015; Friedman 2001).

Perturbation design (discrete ICE grid)

Fix a validation target \(X\) and a reference profile \(z\). The perturbation design constructs \[ \mathcal{G}_X(z)=\{x_{\text{low}},x_{\text{mid}},x_{\text{high}}\}, \] with grid points chosen on the empirical scale of \(X\) computed within the primary stratum \(\mathcal{S}^*\) (country).

For ordered / continuous targets, \[ x_{\text{low}} = Q_{25}(X), \quad x_{\text{mid}} = Q_{50}(X), \quad x_{\text{high}} = Q_{75}(X). \] For binary targets, I use a two-point grid with \(x_{\text{low}}=0\) and \(x_{\text{high}}=1\).

Because mapping and clipping can shrink the effective change between grid points, I track the realised perturbation size \[ \Delta_X = x_{\text{high}}-x_{\text{low}}, \] and its standardised shock size \[ \Delta z_X = \frac{\Delta_X}{\sigma_X}, \] where \(\sigma_X\) is the within-country standard deviation on the analysis scale (for binary \(X\), \(\sigma_X=\sqrt{p(1-p)}\) with \(p=\Pr(X=1)\)).

Perturbation effect definition

Fix an outcome \(Y\), a target \(X\), a profile \(z\), and a prompting strategy \(s\). Let \(f_{\text{ref}}(x,z)\) denote the fitted backbone prediction for \(Y\) on the declared scale when \(X=x\) and all non-target covariates are held at \(z\). Let \(f_{\text{llm}}^{(s)}(x,z)\) denote the LLM’s expected output on the same scale under strategy \(s\), approximated by the sample mean over \(K\) generations.

The reference perturbation effect is \[ PE_{\text{ref}}(X;z)=f_{\text{ref}}(x_{\text{high}},z)-f_{\text{ref}}(x_{\text{low}},z), \] and the LLM perturbation effect is \[ PE_{\text{llm}}^{(s)}(X;z)=f_{\text{llm}}^{(s)}(x_{\text{high}},z)-f_{\text{llm}}^{(s)}(x_{\text{low}},z). \] To compare magnitudes across heterogeneous target scales, I also report standardised effects (per 1 SD on \(X\)) using the realised shock size \(\Delta z_X\): \[ PE_{\text{ref,std}}(X;z)=\frac{PE_{\text{ref}}(X;z)}{\Delta z_X},\qquad PE_{\text{llm,std}}^{(s)}(X;z)=\frac{PE_{\text{llm}}^{(s)}(X;z)}{\Delta z_X}. \]

Why there are two sets: active vs containment, and what each is used for

A single perturbation cell \((X,z)\) can be uninformative for strength comparisons if the backbone effect is essentially zero. I therefore split perturbation cells into two sets that support different claims:

  • The containment set \(\mathcal{S}_0\) is used to quantify false movement: the LLM should not invent sizeable effects when the backbone predicts negligible movement.
  • The active set \(\mathcal{S}_1\) is used to evaluate counterfactual fidelity: when the backbone moves, the LLM should move in the same direction and preserve effect strength.

Formally, \[ \mathcal{S}_0=\{(X,z): |PE_{\text{ref,std}}(X;z)|\le \epsilon_{\text{ref}}\}, \qquad \mathcal{S}_1=\{(X,z): |PE_{\text{ref,std}}(X;z)|> \epsilon_{\text{ref}}\}, \] with main configuration \(\epsilon_{\text{ref}}=0.03\) on the standardised scale.

Active-set diagnostics (\(\mathcal{S}_1\)): direction, ordering, magnitude

  • Directional Agreement (DA). On a non-tie set, \[ DA^{(s)}(X;z)=\mathbb{1}\!\left\{\operatorname{sign}\!\big(PE_{\text{llm}}^{(s)}(X;z)\big) =\operatorname{sign}\!\big(PE_{\text{ref}}(X;z)\big)\right\}. \] No-signal cases (intervals containing zero) are classified using the uncertainty operators in sec-03-shared-machinery and reported explicitly. Aggregating \(DA^{(s)}(X;z)\) over \(\mathcal{S}_1\) yields a Directional Agreement Rate (DAR) with uncertainty intervals.

  • Strength ordering via magnitude concordance (Gini). To assess whether the LLM preserves the ordering of effect magnitudes across perturbation cells, I compute Gini on \(|PE_{\text{ref,std}}|\) versus \(|PE_{\text{llm,std}}^{(s)}|\) using the same tie-aware concordance definition given above, yielding \(G_{\text{mag}}\in[-1,1]\) (reported on \(\mathcal{S}_1\)).

  • Magnitude accuracy via zero-baseline MAE skill. To measure absolute accuracy of standardised effects (not just ordering), I report an MAE skill against a no-effect baseline (\(PE\equiv 0\)): \[ \mathrm{skill}_0 =1-\frac{\mathbb{E}\!\left[\left|PE_{\text{llm,std}}^{(s)}-PE_{\text{ref,std}}\right|\right]} {\mathbb{E}\!\left[\left|PE_{\text{ref,std}}\right|\right]}, \] computed over \(\mathcal{S}_1\). Here \(\mathrm{skill}_0=0\) corresponds to predicting zero effect everywhere.

Containment diagnostics (\(\mathcal{S}_0\)): false movement

On \(\mathcal{S}_0\), the target is containment rather than matching a gradient. I report the false-movement rate \[ FM=\Pr\!\left(\left|PE_{\text{llm,std}}^{(s)}\right|>\epsilon_{\text{move}} \ \middle|\ (X,z)\in\mathcal{S}_0\right), \] with \(\epsilon_{\text{move}}=k\,\epsilon_{\text{ref}}\) (main configuration \(k=3\)), alongside the median \(|PE_{\text{llm,std}}^{(s)}|\) within \(\mathcal{S}_0\).

All perturbation diagnostics are computed at the profile level and summarised by the median within each outcome \(Y\) and target \(X\) reporting cell.

When a three-point grid is available, I additionally compute segment-level agreement. Writing \(\Delta_{L\to M}^{(\text{ref})}\) and \(\Delta_{L\to M}^{(\text{llm})}\) for the low-to-mid contrasts and \(\Delta_{M\to H}^{(\text{ref})}\), \(\Delta_{M\to H}^{(\text{llm})}\) for the mid-to-high contrasts, \[ \begin{aligned} DA_{L\to M}^{(s)}(X;z) &= \mathbb{1}\!\left\{\operatorname{sign}\!\big(\Delta_{L\to M}^{(\text{llm})}\big)=\operatorname{sign}\!\big(\Delta_{L\to M}^{(\text{ref})}\big)\right\},\\ DA_{M\to H}^{(s)}(X;z) &= \mathbb{1}\!\left\{\operatorname{sign}\!\big(\Delta_{M\to H}^{(\text{llm})}\big)=\operatorname{sign}\!\big(\Delta_{M\to H}^{(\text{ref})}\big)\right\}. \end{aligned} \] Curvature is summarised by the discrete second difference \[ \kappa^{(f)}(X;z) = f(x_{\text{high}},z) - 2f(x_{\text{mid}},z) + f(x_{\text{low}},z), \] computed for both the backbone and the LLM to flag strong shape mismatches that can make a two-point contrast misleading.

Block-level diagnostics: sensitivity alignment (perturbed) and importance alignment (unperturbed)

Beyond local perturbations on individual predictors, I test whether the LLM preserves (a) the relative counterfactual sensitivity of predictor blocks under matched perturbations, and (b) predictive reliance across blocks under the observed joint distribution.

For block sensitivity alignment (perturbed), let \(b\in\mathcal{B}\) index predictor blocks and define the standardised block-wise perturbation magnitudes \[ m_{b,\text{ref}}(z)=\big|PE_{\text{ref,std}}(b;z)\big|, \qquad m_{b,\text{llm}}^{(s)}(z)=\big|PE_{\text{llm,std}}^{(s)}(b;z)\big|. \] With medians over reference profiles, \[ \tilde m_{b,\text{ref}}=\operatorname{median}_{z\in\mathcal{Z}} m_{b,\text{ref}}(z),\qquad \tilde m_{b,\text{llm}}^{(s)}=\operatorname{median}_{z\in\mathcal{Z}} m_{b,\text{llm}}^{(s)}(z). \] Block Sensitivity Alignment (BSA) is the Spearman rank correlation between \(\left(\tilde m_{b,\text{ref}}\right)_{b\in\mathcal{B}}\) and \(\left(\tilde m_{b,\text{llm}}^{(s)}\right)_{b\in\mathcal{B}}\), supplemented with Top-\(k\) overlap and highest-quartile concordance.

For block importance alignment (unperturbed), I compute LLM reliance by prompt ablation and compare it to reference reliance via LOBO. For each outcome \(Y\), first compute unperturbed performance under the full prompt \((G_{\text{full}}, \mathrm{skill}_{\text{full}})\). Then, for each block \(b\), remove block \(b\) from the prompt and recompute \((G_{\text{KO}(b)}, \mathrm{skill}_{\text{KO}(b)})\). The LLM block-importance scores are the corresponding performance drops: \[ I_{b,G}^{\text{llm}}(Y)=G_{\text{full}}(Y)-G_{\text{KO}(b)}(Y),\qquad I_{b,\mathrm{skill}}^{\text{llm}}(Y)=\mathrm{skill}_{\text{full}}(Y)-\mathrm{skill}_{\text{KO}(b)}(Y). \] On the reference side, block importance is defined as the drop in pseudo-\(R^2\) when refitting the backbone model with block \(b\) removed: \[ I_b^{\text{ref}}(Y) =\Delta \mathrm{p}R^2_b(Y) =\mathrm{p}R^2_{\text{full}}(Y)-\mathrm{p}R^2_{\text{LOBO}(b)}(Y), \] where \(\mathrm{p}R^2\) is the outcome-appropriate pseudo-\(R^2\) computed on the held-out set. Larger \(\Delta \mathrm{p}R^2_b\) indicates greater predictive reliance on block \(b\). This is a reliance diagnostic, not a causal effect. Importance alignment is summarised, per outcome, by a scatter of \(I_b^{\text{ref}}(Y)\) versus \(I_{b,\cdot}^{\text{llm}}(Y)\) across blocks and a rank correlation across blocks (headline “same blocks” diagnostic), supplemented with Top-\(k\) and highest-quartile concordance.

3.3.5 Belief-update micro-validation

This component evaluates LLMs as a constrained state-update kernel inside the ABM. Given a profile state \(z\) and a controlled informational or social exposure \(e\), the LLM implements \[ U_\phi:\ (z,e)\ \mapsto\ z', \] where \(z'\) is returned on bounded, survey-aligned construct scales. Because the empirical base is cross-sectional, there are no observed trajectories to fit. The objective is therefore micro-level plausibility: updates should respect a small set of invariants that are sufficient for safe simulation use.

Profiles and mutable state

Each belief-update prompt contains a fixed context block treated as non-mutable (e.g., country and basic demographics). The kernel may modify only a predefined mutable construct vector \[ z = (z_{\text{trust}}, z_{\text{legit}}, z_{\text{risk}}, z_{\text{stakes}}, z_{\text{norms}}, z_{\text{constraints}}, z_{\text{ingroup}}), \] corresponding to institutional trust, legitimacy/process scepticism, vaccine-risk perceptions, perceived disease stakes, vaccination norms, perceived constraints/actionability, and group-based trust around vaccination. All other profile attributes are held fixed.

Signals and expected response structure

Signals are drawn from a structured catalogue that makes the expected response pattern explicit. Each signal specifies:

  • which constructs are intended to move (primary targets, optionally secondary/weak targets),
  • which constructs should remain stable (“none” targets, plus optional explicit controls),
  • the intended direction of change on each targeted construct (up/down/none),
  • paired intensity variants (mild vs strong),
  • optional moderation expectations (inequalities that should hold across moderator tiers),
  • an expected relative sensitivity structure encoded as target tiers (primary/secondary/weak/none).

The full machine-readable signal specification is provided as a JSON catalogue.1

Updates, bounds, and scale normalization

Let \(\Delta(z,e)=z'-z\) denote the within-prompt update vector. Each construct is evaluated on its bounded survey-aligned range; out-of-range outputs are treated as boundedness violations.

To compare magnitudes across constructs with different spans, updates are also expressed on a range-normalized scale: \[ |\Delta_v|_{\text{norm}}=\frac{|\Delta_v|}{hi_v-lo_v}, \] when bounds \((lo_v,hi_v)\) are available.

Near-zero movement is handled through a tolerance \(\epsilon\) on the normalized scale. Unless stated otherwise, a construct is treated as meaningfully moved only when \[ |\Delta_v|_{\text{norm}}>\epsilon,\qquad \epsilon=0.03, \] which corresponds to small shifts on the dominant bounded belief scales and prevents scoring “direction” on effectively indeterminate changes.

Core invariants and metrics

Validation follows three invariants aligned with ABM-relevant failure modes:

Scope (containment).

Intended constructs should change while constructs designated as “none” (and explicit controls) remain stable. We report:

  • none-stability rate: share of “none” constructs with \(|\Delta_v|_{\text{norm}}\le \epsilon\),
  • control-stability rate: share of control constructs with \(|\Delta_v|_{\text{norm}}\le \epsilon\),
  • drift on none: mean of \(|\Delta_v|_{\text{norm}}\) over “none” constructs.

Dose response.

Stronger variants should not yield weaker responses than mild variants. Mild–strong monotonicity is evaluated on paired profile prompts for constructs expected to move (direction \(\neq\) none) using \[ |\Delta^{\text{strong}}_v|\ \ge\ |\Delta^{\text{mild}}_v| + m, \] with a small non-negative margin \(m\) to avoid counting negligible numerical differences as violations. The dose-response score is the fraction of tested pairs satisfying the inequality, aggregated across expected-moving constructs.

Directional sense, separated from activation.

Apparent sign failures can arise either from moving in the wrong direction or from producing no detectable movement. Directional scoring is therefore conditional on activation.

Let \(T\) denote the set of primary targets for a signal. Define a prompt as activated on the primary targets if any primary target clears the tolerance: \[ \text{mover}_T = \mathbb{1}\Big[\ \exists v\in T:\ |\Delta_v|_{\text{norm}}>\epsilon\ \Big]. \] We report:

  • Activation (primary): \(\mathbb{E}[\text{mover}_T]\) across prompts for the signal.
  • Sign(primary | mover): sign agreement on primary targets computed only on prompts with \(\text{mover}_T=1\).

This separation makes “wrong-way movement” distinguishable from attenuation/indeterminacy.

Strength alignment via Kendall \(\tau_b\)

Many signals also encode an expected relative sensitivity structure across constructs through tier labels (primary/secondary/weak/none). For each prompt row, tiers are mapped to expected strength scores \[ s_v \in \{0,1,2,3\}\quad\text{for}\quad \{\text{none},\text{weak},\text{secondary},\text{primary}\}. \] We then compare the expected ordering implied by \(s_v\) to the observed ordering of \(|\Delta_v|\) across constructs using Kendall’s \(\tau_b\). This strength alignment score is computed per prompt row and summarised within signals and within primary constructs.

Movement–containment frontier and ABM lever typology

To translate diagnostics into ABM-ready levers, each signal is positioned on a movement–containment plane using range-normalized magnitudes:

  • \(x\) (primary movement): mean of \(|\Delta_v|_{\text{norm}}\) over primary targets,
  • \(y\) (off-target drift): mean of \(|\Delta_v|_{\text{norm}}\) over “none” constructs.

Signals are then classified with interpretability anchors:

  • minimum meaningful primary movement: \(x_0 = 0.125\),
  • drift anchor: \(y_0 = 0.03\),
  • leakage slope: \(\lambda = y_0/x_0 = 0.24\),
  • spillover ceiling: \(y_{\max}=0.06\).

A signal is treated as “safe” with respect to scope control when \[ x \ge x_0 \quad\text{and}\quad y \le \min(\lambda x,\ y_{\max}). \] This yields four regimes used as an ABM shock library:

  • Safe targeted shock: \(x\ge x_0\) and satisfies the safe boundary.
  • Leaky shock: \(x\ge x_0\) but violates proportional leakage (\(y>\lambda x\)) or the ceiling (\(y>y_{\max}\)).
  • Cross-talk / reinterpretation: \(x<x_0\) and \(y\ge y_0\) (broad motion without a dominant primary lever).
  • Stable low-signal: \(x<x_0\) and \(y<y_0\) (contained but weak).

Model/strategy selection and uncertainty summaries

Belief-update kernels are compared across models and prompting strategies using a gate score that prioritises ABM safety and shape constraints: \[ \text{gate} = \text{none-stability} + \text{control-stability} + \text{mild–strong monotonicity}. \] Unless stated otherwise, signal-level summaries pool mild and strong variants, while model–strategy frontiers are summarised by robust across-signal statistics. Uncertainty summaries are reported via bootstrap with \(B=500\), aligned to the level of aggregation used in the corresponding table or figure.

Behavioural implications under the choice model

Finally, belief updates are connected to behaviour by composing the update kernel with the survey-anchored choice mapping used elsewhere in the thesis. For each updated state \(z'\), we evaluate the implied behavioural change \(\Delta y\) under the choice model and check whether the direction and relative pattern of \(\Delta y\) agree with the choice gradients implied by the statistical backbone for the constructs targeted by the signal.
