2  Introduction & Research Motivation

Designing good policy is fundamentally a counterfactual exercise: we need to know what would happen if a specific intervention were introduced. Yet clean counterfactual evidence is rare. Real-world systems resist experimental manipulation, and observational studies often lack the external validity needed to predict outcomes in complex, interactive environments. Agent-based models (ABMs) offer a way forward. They enable in silico experiments that trace outcomes across heterogeneous populations where interaction effects matter. They can also represent feedback between epidemic conditions and behaviour: risk signals shift decisions, decisions shift uptake and protection, and the resulting epidemiological pressure reshapes the signals that triggered the response.

Behavioural ABMs, however, face a recurring difficulty. Aggregate trajectories can be highly sensitive to how individual decision rules are specified and to how psychological profile variables evolve over time. Building models that are both parsimonious and realistic demands substantial modelling effort and non-trivial calibration. Worse, macro-level fit alone does not identify the operative micro-level structure, because distinct update rules can generate similar aggregate trajectories.

Large language models (LLMs) introduce a recent possibility in this space. Because they produce context-sensitive outputs from rich descriptions of individuals, they can serve as candidate behavioural components both for mapping survey-grounded profiles into actions and for updating parts of those profiles under information exposure and social signals. A growing line of work explores ABMs with LLM-driven agents, and evaluation proposals are emerging. Validation, however, is typically one-shot and capability-oriented, prioritising plausibility and baseline fit over whether a model responds sensibly to interventions or remains stable when iterated. This leaves an open problem: how to build an auditable, empirically anchored bridge between empirical evidence, generative behavioural components, and counterfactual ABM simulation.

Preventive behaviour in epidemics is well suited to this agenda because the main policy targets are interpretable and because the large availability of cross-sectional data on these behavioural processes makes it possible to anchor key parts of the modelling problem. In particular, such data can anchor both the distribution of survey-grounded respondent profiles in the population and the empirical mapping from those profiles to preventive choices. The same data are less informative about how those profiles evolve over time, which is precisely where counterfactual simulation becomes most fragile. This makes preventive behaviour a useful domain in which to connect empirical evidence, evaluation of LLMs, and ABM design within a single framework.

The thesis therefore proceeds in three linked steps.

First, I ask whether prevention decisions can be organised by a compact empirical structure that jointly accounts for multiple behavioural outcomes and can be generalised across countries. Using data from a large cross-country survey, I reduce a high-dimensional questionnaire to a smaller set of theory-guided blocks, including perceived disease stakes, institutional trust and legitimacy, norms, disposition, moral orientation, constraints, and habit. I then use these blocks to identify the main correlates of preventive behaviour and to extract empirical gradients that can anchor downstream modelling.

Second, I examine whether LLMs can reproduce this survey-grounded structure in a way that is useful for simulation. To do so, I differentiate between two tasks. The first is a behavioural-outcome task, in which the model maps a fixed survey-grounded respondent profile to a preventive action. The second is a psychological-profile task, in which the model reconstructs or perturbs psychological profile variables from broader survey information and controlled shifts in profile-relevant cues. The results reveal an asymmetry in how current LLM-as-agent approaches fail. In the behavioural-outcome task, models often recover the correct ordering of preventive propensity, but they miss on magnitude: predictions regress toward the mean and compress behavioural variation. In the psychological-profile task, the failure reverses. Responses become too diffuse and too volatile: models overreact to single cues, fail to preserve inertia, and shift dimensions that should have remained comparatively stable. For this reason, current LLMs are not reliable drop-in components for disciplined counterfactual simulation.

Guided by these diagnostic failures, the final step moves away from using LLMs to drive behavioural updating over time. Instead, the ABM keeps behaviour anchored to the survey: it starts from survey-grounded respondent profiles and uses the estimated empirical relationships between those profiles and preventive outcomes. Counterfactual policies are introduced as changes in the conditions people face, and the simulation tracks how behaviour evolves over time under inertia, social influence, and epidemic feedback.

In brief, once the behavioural core is kept survey-grounded, the ABM suggests that different intervention regimes can produce different dynamic signatures. Credibility-repair interventions tend to generate gradual but durable shifts, whereas risk-salience shocks are sharper but more short-lived. Norm-based interventions can persist because exposure and visibility accumulate over time. By contrast, when policy targets vaccination alone, the resulting drop in perceived risk can also weaken other precautions through feedback between protection and infection pressure.

The remainder of the thesis follows this progression. sec-02-literature-review situates the project in the literature on preventive behaviour, behavioural modelling, and ABMs, including recent work on LLM-driven agents. sec-03-decision-function, sec-04-llm-micro-validation, and sec-05-agent-based-model implement the empirical, evaluative, and simulation steps outlined above. sec-06-discussion-conclusion summarises implications, limitations, and directions for hybrid architectures.
